Rulkov Model

The Rulkov map is a two-dimensional iterated map used for modeling biological neurons. Proposed by Nikolai F. Rulkov in 2001, this map offers computational advantages when studying neural networks. Its simplicity in iteration compared to continuous dynamical systems reduces memory usage and facilitates the computation of large-scale neural networks.

The Model

The Rulkov map, expressed with \(n\) as discrete time, is defined by the following equations: \[ \begin{align} x_{n + 1} &= \frac{\alpha}{1 + x_{n}^2} + y_{n}, \\ y_{n + 1} &= y_{n} - \mu (x_{n} - \sigma). \end{align} \] Here, \(x\) represents the membrane potential of the neuron, and \(y\) is a slow variable, characterized by the small value of \(\mu\) (\(0 < \mu \ll 1\)). Although \(y\) lacks explicit biological meaning, it may be analogized to gating variables. The parameter \(\sigma\) acts as an external direct current input to the neuron, while \(\alpha\) serves as the map's nonlinearity parameter. Various combinations of \(\sigma\) and \(\alpha\) yield distinct neuronal states, such as resting, tonic spiking, and chaotic bursting, the latter occurring when \(\alpha > 4\).

Analysis

The dynamics of the Rulkov map can be studied by examining the behavior of its one-dimensional fast submap. Since \(y\) evolves slowly, it can be approximated as a constant in \(x\)'s evolution equation over moderate time spans. Depending on the value of \(y\), the submap may have one or three fixed points. These include a stable point, an unstable point, and a third point whose stability can vary. As \(y\) increases, the stable and unstable fixed points merge and disappear via a saddle-node bifurcation.

Coupling

The coupling of two neurons has been explored by Irina Bashkirtseva and Alexander Pisarchik. Their studies reveal transitions between stationary, periodic, quasiperiodic, and chaotic regimes. They also investigated how random disturbances influence the system, leading to noise-induced transitions between periodic and chaotic stochastic oscillations.

Other Applications

Variants of the Rulkov map have been applied in fields like labor and industrial economics, particularly in modeling corporate dynamics. These adaptations utilize synchronization and chaos regulation to explain transitions among multiple equilibria, incorporate idiosyncratic elements, and analyze the impact of effort on corporate profitability. Orlando and Bufalo introduced a deterministic model based on the Rulkov map, effectively modeling fluctuations in corporate yields and spreads during periods of distress, such as the COVID-19 pandemic. When compared to the ARIMA-EGARCH model, which handles various aspects of volatility, the deterministic Rulkov map yields comparable results. Its deterministic nature may enhance explanatory power in specific contexts.
